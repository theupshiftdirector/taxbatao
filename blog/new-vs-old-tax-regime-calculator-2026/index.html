What Changed in the New Tax Regime for FY 2025-26?

The new tax regime, introduced in Budget 2020, continues to be more attractive for salaried employees and business owners in the financial year 2025-26 (Assessment Year 2026-27). Under the new regime, you get lower tax rates but cannot claim deductions under sections 80C, 80D, 80CCD, and other exemptions.

For FY 2025-26, the new tax regime offers significantly lower tax rates. A salaried individual earning ₹10 lakhs per year would pay approximately ₹98,000 in tax under the new regime versus ₹1,23,500 under the old regime (assuming standard deductions and 80C investments). This represents a 20% tax saving for most middle-income earners.

The Government of India has made the new regime the default option for most taxpayers from Assessment Year 2024-25 onwards. However, you still have the choice to opt for the old regime if you have substantial deductions under 80C, 80D, and other sections.

The real advantage of the old regime emerges when you have substantial deductions under section 80C (₹1.5 lakh limit), section 80D (health insurance premiums), and section 80CCD (NPS contributions up to ₹2 lakh). If you invest in Life Insurance, PPF, ELSS, or National Savings Certificates (NSC), these reduce your taxable income only in the old regime.

For example, if you earn ₹15 lakhs annually and invest ₹1.5 lakh under 80C, your taxable income under the old regime becomes ₹13.5 lakhs. Under the new regime, your taxable income remains ₹15 lakhs. This makes a difference of approximately ₹22,500 in tax liability for high-income earners with significant deductions.

When Should You Choose the Old Tax Regime?

The old regime is beneficial if you:

  • Invest regularly in 80C instruments: If you contribute ₹1.5 lakh annually to PPF, ELSS, or Life Insurance policies
  • Have medical expenses: If you or your family members have substantial medical insurance premiums (80D deduction up to ₹25,000 per year)
  • Contribute to NPS: If you're making voluntary NPS contributions beyond the mandatory 10% (additional ₹2 lakh under 80CCD(1B))
  • Earn above ₹15 lakhs: Higher earners benefit more from deductions due to the compounding effect of tax slabs
  • Self-employed or running a business: Business owners often have more deduction opportunities

Practical Examples: Real-World Tax Calculations for 2025-26

Example 1: Middle-Income Salaried Employee

Rajesh earns ₹10 lakhs annually. He invests ₹1.5 lakh in ELSS (80C) and pays ₹10,000 health insurance premium (80D).

  • Old Regime: Taxable income = ₹10,00,000 - ₹50,000 (standard) - ₹1,50,000 (80C) - ₹10,000 (80D) = ₹7,90,000. Tax = ₹1,04,500
  • New Regime: Taxable income = ₹10,00,000 - ₹50,000 (standard) = ₹9,50,000. Tax = ₹98,000
  • Difference: Old regime saves ₹6,500 annually

Example 2: High-Income Professional with Maximum Deductions

Priya earns ₹25 lakhs per year. She invests ₹1.5 lakh (80C), pays ₹15,000 health insurance (80D), contributes ₹2 lakh to NPS (80CCD), and has ₹3 lakh home loan interest (24).

  • Old Regime: Taxable income = ₹25,00,000 - ₹50,000 - ₹1,50,000 - ₹15,000 - ₹2,00,000 - ₹3,00,000 = ₹17,85,000. Tax = ₹4,10,750
  • New Regime: Taxable income = ₹25,00,000 - ₹50,000 = ₹24,50,000. Tax = ₹6,15,000
  • Difference: Old regime saves ₹2,04,250 annually (33% savings!)

These examples demonstrate that higher earners with substantial deductions benefit significantly from the old regime. However, most salaried employees earning under ₹12 lakhs benefit from the new regime.

Important Changes and Key Takeaways for 2026

Key Points to Remember:

  • The new regime became the default from AY 2024-25 onwards. You must actively opt for the old regime if you want it.
  • No deduction under 80C, 80D, 80CCD, 24, or 80G in the new regime. You get lower rates instead.
  • Standard deduction of ₹50,000 is available in both regimes for salaried employees.
  • Surcharge and cess still apply in both regimes for high earners. Surcharge is 10% on income above ₹50 lakhs and 15% above ₹1 crore.
  • Health insurance deduction (80D) is not available in the new regime, making it unfavorable for families with high medical costs.
  • NPS contributions are irrelevant in new regime for tax purposes, though still beneficial for retirement planning.
  • Once you opt for the old regime, you can switch back to new regime only after a gap of 5 years (from AY 2024-25 rules).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch between old and new tax regime every year?

No. If you opt for the old regime in any financial year, you must continue with it for at least 4 consecutive years. From AY 2024-25 onwards, once you choose the old regime, you can switch back to the new regime only after 5 years. This is per Income Tax Rules 115BAC. Plan carefully before making this choice.

Is life insurance premium under section 80C deductible in the new tax regime?

No. Section 80C deductions (including life insurance, PPF, ELSS, NSC, and tuition fees) are not available under the new tax regime. The new regime offers lower tax rates as compensation for losing these deductions. This is a major disadvantage for investors who prioritize tax-advantaged saving schemes.

What is the maximum 80CCD(1B) deduction for NPS in 2025-26?

The maximum additional deduction under 80CCD(1B) is ₹2 lakh per financial year, which can be claimed only in the old regime. This applies to any taxpayer contributing to a National Pension System account. However, the basic 80CCD(1) deduction limit of ₹1.5 lakh is included in the overall ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit.

Does home loan interest deduction apply to the new tax regime?

No. Home loan interest deduction under section 24 (maximum ₹2 lakh) applies only to the old regime. First-time homebuyers should strongly consider the old regime if they have significant home loan interest payments. Self-occupied property interest is deductible, but let-out property interest has no limit.

What is the difference between old regime surcharge for FY 2025-26?

Surcharge rates are identical in both regimes. If your income exceeds ₹50 lakhs, you pay 10% surcharge. Above ₹1 crore, it's 15%. Above ₹2 crores, it's 25%, and above ₹5 crores, it's 37%.
